## Question 1 (Year: 2018 | Paper: GS III)
**Question ID: 2018-gs3-q1-civilsdaily**

**How are the principles followed by NITI Aayog different from those followed by the erstwhile planning commission in India? [Year: 2018] [Marks: 15]**

### Answer

NITI Aayog, established in **2015**, replaced the Planning Commission to reflect India's transition from a **centralized planned economy** to a **market-led, cooperative federalist model**.

### Key Differences Between Planning Commission and NITI Aayog

| **Planning Commission** | **NITI Aayog** |
| --- | --- |
| Followed **centralized planning** based on command-control economic principles. | Follows **market-driven**, **evidence-based**, **reform-oriented** principles. |
| Practiced **top-down federalism** | Promotes **cooperative and competitive federalism** |
| **Allocation of funds** to states | Acts as a **think tank**, does not allocate funds. |
| Emphasis on **Five-Year Plans**, rigid targets. | Emphasis on **long-term vision**, **strategic reforms**, **outcome monitoring** (e.g., SDG Index). |
| **Monolithic**, bureaucratic, centralized by design. | **Flexible**, with involvement of experts, domain specialists, and states. |
| **one-size-fits-all** approach. | Tailored strategies. Eg- **Aspirational Districts Programme** |
| Limited focus on real-time evaluation | Strong focus on **data-driven governance**, real-time dashboards. |
| Minimal involvement of private sector, academia, civil society. | Encourages **multi-stakeholder consultations** for policy formulation. |

### Similarities Between NITI Aayog and Planning Commission

1. **National Development Objective**
2. **Advisory Role to Government**
3. **Coordination Function**
4. **Focus on Long-term Vision**
5. **Multisectoral Engagement**

### Importance of NITI Aayog

1. **Reflects shift from state-led to market-led development model**
2. **Improves Centre-State cooperation for faster execution**
3. **Enhances accountability and outcome-based governance**
4. **Encourages policy experimentation and innovation**

NITI Aayog reflects India's evolving needs as a **21st-century, globally integrated economy**.

## Question 2 (Year: 2020 | Paper: GS III)
**Question ID: 2020-gs3-q2-civilsdaily**

**Define potential GDP and explain its determinants. What are the factors that have been inhibiting India from realizing its potential GDP? [Year: 2020] [Marks: 10]**

### Answer

**Potential GDP** refers to the **maximum sustainable output** an economy can produce **without generating inflationary pressure**, when all resources are fully and efficiently employed.

### Determinants of Potential GDP

1. **Labour Force & Human Capital** - Size, skill, and productivity of the workforce.
2. **Capital Formation** - Investment in infrastructure, machinery, and technology.
3. **Technology & Innovation** - R&D and digital transformation driving productivity.
4. **Institutional Quality** - Governance, regulatory efficiency, and property rights.
5. **Total Factor Productivity (TFP)** - Efficiency in using labour and capital together.
6. **Prevailing Inflation Rate** - Persistent inflation distorts real GDP from its potential level.
7. **Global Conditions** - Protectionism, trade restrictions, and geopolitical tensions. Eg- Tariff Wars

### Factors Inhibiting India from Realizing Potential GDP

1. **Low Female Labour Force Participation** - FLFPR only **41.7% (PLFS)** against global average of 48%
2. **Slow Capital Formation** - GFCF at **~29.6% of GDP (2024) vs 34% in 2023**.
3. **Skill Mismatch & Education Gaps** - Only **4.7% of workforce formally skilled (NSDC)**.
4. **Infrastructure Bottlenecks** - Logistics cost **~13% of GDP** vs 8% in USA
5. **Weak Productivity Growth** - Low TFP and informal sector dominance. (83% informal sector)
6. **Regulatory Cholesterol** - Delays, compliance burden, weak contract enforcement.

### Way Forward

1. **Enhance Human Capital** - Invest in **education, healthcare, and skill development**
2. **Accelerate Investment & Infrastructure Growth** through faster project execution under PPP.
3. Create **safe workplaces, flexible jobs, and childcare support** to tap women's economic potential.
4. Increase **R&D spending to 2.5% of GDP (currently <1% of GDP)** for productivity gains.

To realize its **potential GDP and Viksit Bharat 2047**, India must shift from **factor accumulation** to **productivity-driven growth**.

## Question 3 (Year: 2021 | Paper: GS III)
**Question ID: 2021-gs3-q3-civilsdaily**

**Explain the difference between computing methodology of India's Gross Domestic Product (GDP) before the year 2015 and after the year 2015. [Year: 2021] [Marks: 10]**

### Answer

**Gross Domestic Product (GDP)** is the **total monetary value of all final goods and services** produced within a country's borders in a given period. GDP in India is calculated by the **National Statistical Office (NSO)**.

| **Parameter** | **Before 2015 Methodology** | **After 2015 Methodology** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Base Year** | **2004-05** | **2011-12** (reflecting modern economy) |
| **GDP Measurement** | **GDP at Factor Cost** | **GDP at Market Prices** (includes taxes, subtracts subsidies) |
| **International Standard Followed** | **UN-SNA 1993** | **UN-SNA 2008** |
| **Corporate Sector Data** | ASI + sample surveys | **MCA-21 database** covering 5+ lakh companies |
| **Manufacturing Sector Measurement** | Production-based estimates | **Value Added + Double-Deflation Method** |

| **Price Indices for Real GDP** | Mostly **WPI** | Combination of **CPI + WPI + sectoral indices** |
| **Financial Sector Coverage** | Mostly banks | **NBFCs, Mutual Funds, Insurance, Market data** |
| **Services Sector Coverage** | Limited | **Expanded IT, telecom, real estate, digital services** |
| **Informal Sector Estimation** | Older NSSO data | Updated **NSSO surveys + improved estimation techniques** |

The post-2015 GDP methodology aims to provide a **more accurate, data-rich, and globally comparable** picture of India's economy. To improve reliability, there is a need for greater transparency.

## Question 4 (Year: 2023 | Paper: GS III)
**Question ID: 2023-gs3-q4-civilsdaily**

**Most of the unemployment in India is structural in nature. Examine the methodology adopted to compute unemployment in the country and suggest improvements. [Year: 2023] [Marks: 15]**

### Answer

**Structural unemployment** occurs when workers **lack the skills, education, or geographic mobility** required to match available jobs. In India, it reflects a **mismatch** between the workforce's capabilities and the evolving needs of a modern economy.

### Why Unemployment is Structural in India

1. **Skill Mismatch** - Majority of workforce is low-skilled; only **~4.7% formally skilled** (NSDC).
2. **Agriculture Dependence** - **49% workforce** in agriculture producing **16-17% of GDP**
3. **Slow Growth of Labour-Intensive Industries** - Manufacturing unable to absorb labour at scale.
4. **Automation and Digitalisation** - Eg- AI, Robotics leading to job losses
5. **Low Female Labour Participation** - FLFPR at **41.7% (PLFS 2023-24)** due to social norms, skill gaps, and lack of suitable jobs.
6. **Regional Imbalances** - Job clusters in southern/western India vs labour concentration in BIMARU states.
7. **Informalization of economy** - **89%** of workforce in informal sector.

### Methodology to Compute Unemployment in India

1. **NSSO (under MOSPI)** is the principal body responsible for estimating unemployment.
2. **Periodic Labour Force Survey (PLFS) - NSO** measures unemployment through three indicators:
    - **Usual Status (US/PS+SS)** - Based on activity over **365 days**
    - **Current Weekly Status (CWS)** - If not worked for **1 hour in the last 7 days**.
    - **Current Daily Status (CDS)** - Records activity for each day of last week - best for informal/underemployment.
3. **Household Surveys** - Annual (rural + urban) and quarterly (urban) surveys.
4. **Establishment Surveys**
    - **QES** for formal sector
    - **ASI** for organised manufacturing
5. **Administrative Data - EPFO, ESIC, NPS** payrolls used to estimate formal job creation.
6. Unemployment rate = No. of unemployed persons / Total labour force

### Issues with Current Methodology

1. **Underestimation of Informal Sector** - ~90% workforce informal. PLFS & enterprise surveys do not capture home-based, gig, or platform work fully.
2. Surveys don't map **job requirements vs worker skills**, essential for assessing structural unemployment.
3. **Low Frequency** - Eg- PLFS rural data is measured annually
4. **Urban Bias** - Quarterly surveys are confined to urban areas. Rural distress is under-measured.
5. **Limited Coverage** - Gig economy, digital services, start-ups, and EV/green jobs not adequately represented.

### Way Forward

1. **Use Big Data Analytics** to gather real-time analysis.
2. Incorporate **'underemployment'** into the definition of unemployment.
3. **Timely release of data**.
4. **Increase Frequency** - Monthly or quarterly surveys for rural areas
5. **Align with International Standards (ILO + SNA 2025)** - Update definitions to include **multi-job holders, remote workers, freelancers**, and platform-based workers.

Improving methodology is essential to generate accurate employment estimates and design stronger job creation policies.

## Question 5 (Year: 2023 | Paper: GS III)
**Question ID: 2023-gs3-q5-civilsdaily**

**Distinguish between 'care economy' and 'monetized economy'. How can care economy be brought into monetized economy through women empowerment? [Year: 2023] [Marks: 15]**

### Answer

**Care economy** refers to the **unpaid or underpaid activities** performed within households and communities such as childcare, elderly care, household maintenance, and emotional labour. In contrast, the **monetized economy** includes **all paid economic activities** that generate income.

### Key Features of the Care Economy:

- Human-Centered
- Labour-Intensive
- Majorly Informal Sector

### Fact File

1. The contribution of care work to India's GDP is around 15-17%
2. Women contribute 84% of unpaid care work.
3. Global care investments can create 475 million jobs by 2030.

| **Care Economy** | **Monetized Economy** |
| --- | --- |
| Unpaid caregiving, domestic work, emotional labour | Paid labour producing goods and services |
| Not counted in GDP or national accounts | Direct contributor to GDP and economic growth |
| Largely invisible, undervalued | Highly visible and economically recognised |
| Performed mostly by **women** | Higher participation of **men** |
| Informal, unrecognized skills | Formal skills, training and certifications |
| No wages or financial return | Generates income and employment |
| Seen as natural duty or responsibility | Seen as productive economic activity |

### Bringing Care Economy into Monetized Economy through Women Empowerment

1. **Raising investment** in the care economy to **2% (>1% current)** can generate **11 million jobs** for women.
2. Draw lessons from **Japan's womenomics** for boosting female labour participation.
3. Encourage **PPP models** and **CSR initiatives** to expand affordable care infrastructure.
4. **Skill Development and Certification** in **childcare, geriatric care, nursing, early education, nutrition**.
5. **Formalisation of Care Services** by expanding **creches, Anganwadis, elderly care centres, community caregiving services**.
6. **Promotion of Women-led Care Entrepreneurship** such as **day-care centres, tiffin services** by providing **credit, digital platforms, SHG support, and market linkages**.
7. **Social Protection** - Recognising care work under **minimum wage laws** and **social security frameworks**.
8. Digital platforms like **online caregiving services, home-nursing apps, domestic work registries** help women monetise care skills.
9. Investments in **time-saving infrastructure** such as **clean cooking fuel, piped water, and public transport** can increase FLFPR in the formal economy.

**Adopting the 3R Framework (Recognize, Reduce and Redistribute) can help realise SDG 5.4.**

## Question 6 (Year: 2018 | Paper: GS III)
**Question ID: 2018-gs3-q6-civilsdaily**

**How would the recent phenomena of protectionism and currency manipulations in world trade affect macroeconomic stability of India? [Year: 2018] [Marks: 15]**

### Answer

The rising **protectionism** and **currency manipulations** have disrupted global trade flows and have direct implications for **India's** growth, inflation, fiscal balance, and external vulnerability.

### Tools of Protectionism

1. **Tariffs**
2. **Quotas**
3. **Import Licensing**
4. **Sanctions**
5. **Exchange Controls**
6. **Industrial Subsidies**

### Impact of Protectionism on Macroeconomic Stability of India

1. **Export Slowdown** due to **high tariffs**. Eg: US protectionism under Section 232 hurt India's steel exports.
2. **Supply Chain Disruptions** lead to **higher Production Costs**. Eg- higher oil prices after Israel-Palestine conflict
3. **Imported Inflation** due to barriers on food, energy and intermediate goods. **Eg:** Indonesia palm oil ban.
4. **Weak Employment in Export-oriented Sectors** - Eg: Fall in European demand hit India's **textile and leather clusters**.
5. **Lower FDI Inflows** - Uncertain trade regimes discourage long-term investments. Eg- Apple cancelling plant in India after Trump threat.

### Impact of Currency Manipulations on Macroeconomic Stability

1. **Widening Trade Deficit** - Undervalued currencies make their exports cheaper. Eg- China's managed yuan
2. **Rupee Volatility** creates **monetary Policy Challenges**. Eg: Yen depreciation in 2023-24 triggered pressure on Asian currencies including INR.
3. **Higher Inflation and BoP Pressure** - Eg: INR touching 83-84 per USD raised petroleum import bills.
4. **Capital Outflows** due to dollar strengthening. **Eg:** 2022-24 saw **FPI outflows** during phases of aggressive US Fed tightening.
5. **Pressure on Forex Reserves** - Eg: RBI sold USD in 2022-23 to stabilise INR, reducing reserves temporarily.

### Opportunities for India Amid Protectionism & Currency Politics

1. **China+1 Advantage** in electronics, chemicals, renewables. **Eg-** Mobile exports crossed USD 11 bn in 2023-24.
2. **Boost Make in India** to build self-reliant supply chains. **Eg: PLI schemes** in semiconductors, textiles, solar modules.
3. **Diversification of Trade Partners** - Eg- Recent FTA with UK
4. **Strategic Attractiveness as a Stable Market** - Amid volatile currencies and geo-economic blocs, India is seen as a stable investment destination.
5. **Promoting Rupee Trade Mechanisms** - Eg- INR invoicing and Vostro accounts.
6. **Opportunity to Lead on Fair Trade Norms** in WTO, G20 on currency transparency and non-tariff barriers.

### Way Forward

1. Enhance **R&D (2.5% of GDP)**, reduce **logistics costs (PM Gati Shakti)**, and expand **PLI schemes** to boost manufacturing resilience.
2. Accelerate FTAs with **EU, GCC** to reduce over-dependence on a few partners.
3. Strengthen **FOREX buffers** and expand **rupee trade settlement**
4. Encourage domestic production of **critical inputs (electronics, APIs, green tech)** to reduce vulnerability to global shocks.
5. Scale **IT, fintech, health tourism, education services** to offset goods-trade shocks from rising protectionism.

By strengthening domestic competitiveness, India can position itself as a **reliable, rules-based and resilient player in the evolving global economic order**.

## Question 7 (Year: 2019 | Paper: GS III)
**Question ID: 2019-gs3-q7-civilsdaily**

**Do you agree with the view that steady GDP growth and low inflation have left the Indian economy in good shape? Give reasons in support of your arguments. [Year: 2019] [Marks: 10]**

### Answer

India is projected to sustain GDP growth of **6.5%** between **FY28-30**, positioning it as the world's **third-largest consumer market** by 2026 and the **third-largest economy** by 2028. (UBS)

### Arguments Supporting the View (Indian economy in good shape)

1. **High GDP Growth** - India remains the **fastest-growing major economy**. 7% in FY 2025.
2. **Moderating Inflation** - Eg- Retail inflation fell to a **historic low of 0.25%** in October 2025, due to GST rate cuts
3. **Forex reserves** at over **$689 billion** provide external stability.
4. **Fiscal Consolidation Path** - Fiscal deficit targeted to reduce to **4.8% of GDP in 2025-26**.
5. **Robust Financial Sector** - Gross NPAs have declined from 9.11% (2021) to **2.8% (2025)**.
6. Production-linked incentives (PLI) has raised India's manufacturing attractiveness. **Eg:** Electronics exports at a record **$38 billion in 2024-25**. (32% increase)

### Arguments Against the View (Macro vulnerabilities persist)

1. **Jobless Growth** - Service sector contributes 55% of GDP but employs less than 30% workforce
2. **High food inflation** due to climate shocks, hurting the poor.
3. **Rural Distress** due to weak agriculture real wages and uneven monsoons.
4. **Global slowdown**, protectionism, and China's dominance limit India's merchandise exports.
5. **High Public Debt** - General government debt remains around **82% of GDP (IMF, 2024)**, limiting fiscal room.
6. The share of **Gross Fixed Capital Formation (GFCF)** was about **34.6% of GDP in 2023-24** and slipped to **29.61%** of GDP in 2024, indicating weak investment.

### Way Forward

1. Enhance **R&D (2.5% of GDP)**, reduce **logistics costs (PM Gati Shakti)**, and expand **PLI schemes** to boost manufacturing.
2. Promote **labour-intensive manufacturing** (textiles, toys, food processing) and expand **services exports** (IT, GBS, health tourism).
3. Improve **ease of doing business**, accelerate **contract enforcement**, and reduce regulatory uncertainty to crowd-in private capital.
4. Strengthen **FOREX buffers** and expand **rupee trade settlement**
5. Encourage domestic production of **critical inputs (electronics, APIs, green tech)** to reduce vulnerability to global shocks.

As highlighted by the Economic Survey, India must prioritise **blue-sky thinking** and foster a **virtuous cycle of investment** to achieve Viksit Bharat@2047.

## Question 8 (Year: 2022 | Paper: GS III)
**Question ID: 2022-gs3-q8-civilsdaily**

**"Economic growth in the recent past has been led by increase in labour activity." Explain this statement. Suggest the growth pattern that will lead to creation of more jobs without compromising labour productivity. [Year: 2022] [Marks: 15]**

### Answer

With **7% growth in 2025-26**, India is one of the **fastest-growing major economies** and a **bright spot** on the global economy **(IMF)**. A major driver of this performance has been the **expansion in labour activity**.

### Economic growth attributed to labour activity

1. **Demographic dividend** - **Median age of 28** and **65% working-age population (65%)** has increased labour supply and productive capacity.
2. **Shift towards labour-intensive sectors**: Growth in **construction, retail, transportation, tourism, gig and platform economy**
3. **Surge in self-employment** - **from 52% (2017) to 58% in 2024 (PLFS data)**
4. **The government's skilling push** through **Kaushal Vikas Yojana** and the **Skill India Mission** improved workforce capabilities.
5. India becoming the **3rd largest start-up ecosystem** has generated new entrepreneurship-led employment.
6. **Rise of gig economy** - Platform-based work has widened job opportunities.
7. **Labour Code reforms** - consolidation of labour laws has improved **hiring flexibility and EoDB.**

### Other reasons

- **GST reforms**
- **Ease of Doing Business** reforms
- **IBC**
- **PLI schemes**

### However, this growth pattern is problematic due to

1. **Low productivity trap**: Most new jobs are in **informal, low-wage, low-productivity** sectors.
2. **Disguised employment rising**: Higher labour supply masks **underemployment**.
3. **Limited wage growth**: High labour participation has not translated into better wages.
4. **Structural transformation incomplete**: Manufacturing's share in jobs and GDP remains stagnant.

### Suggested Growth Pattern to Create More Jobs Without Compromising Productivity

1. **Manufacturing-led, technology-enabled growth**
    - Expand **labour-intensive manufacturing** such as **textiles, toys, leather, electronics assembly**. **Eg: PLI schemes** for electronics, textiles.
    - Use **AI, robotics, lean production** to improve productivity while expanding scale.

2. **MSME upgradation** - Enable **cluster-based development**, digitalisation, easier credit. **Eg: MSME Champions Scheme, ONDC for market linkages**
3. **Skill-based job creation** through programs like **Skill India, PMKVY 4.0**.
4. Boost **food processing, millets, horticulture**, and **FPO-based value chains**.
5. Employment in **solar manufacturing, EV ecosystem, recycling, energy efficiency** can raise both **jobs** and **productivity**.
6. **Strengthen urban employment ecosystems** - Invest in **urban infrastructure**, housing, logistics, and city industrial clusters.
7. Improve **FLFPR** through childcare support, flexible work, safety, and skilling.

India's recent growth has been driven more by **labour mobilisation** than by **labour productivity**. A shift towards **manufacturing-led, technology-driven, and green growth** is essential for **Viksit Bharat 2047**.

## Question 10 (Year: 2019 | Paper: GS III)
**Question ID: 2019-gs3-q10-civilsdaily**

**It is argued that the strategy of inclusive growth is intended to meet the objectives of inclusiveness and sustainability together. Comment on this statement. [Year: 2019] [Marks: 15]**

### Answer

As per **OECD**, inclusive growth is economic growth distributed fairly across society and creates opportunities for all.

### Fact File

1. **Extreme poverty** fell from **16.2% in 2011-12 to just 2.3% in 2022-23**

### Inclusive Growth Promoting Inclusiveness

1. **Expands economic opportunities** with focus on **education, health, skilling**, and access to markets. Eg-PM-JANMAN for tribal inclusion.
2. **Balanced regional growth** with targeted interventions.
3. **Income security** - Social protection systems like **MGNREGA**, NFSA, PM-KISAN reduce vulnerability and support inclusive livelihoods.
4. **Strengthens financial inclusion** - Eg- **PM Jan Dhan Yojana** opened **500 million+ bank accounts**
5. **Equality of Opportunity** - Eg- the **Rights of Persons with Disabilities (RPwD) Act, 2016**
6. **Ayushman Bharat**: Provided **free healthcare to 23 crore people**.

### Inclusive Growth Ensuring Sustainability

1. Affordable and clean energy (SDG 7) - **PM Ujjwala Yojana** distributed **10 crore LPG connections**
2. Encourages **sustainable consumption and production** patterns (SDG 12). Eg- Mission LiFE
3. Supports protection of natural resources-forests, soil, and biodiversity (SDG 15). **Eg:** Compensatory Afforestation Funds
4. **Sustainable Livelihoods** - Promotes climate-resilient agriculture, water conservation, and diversified livelihoods.
5. **Institutional Sustainability (SDG 16, SDG 17)** through decentralisation, cooperative federalism and data-driven governance. Eg- Aspirational Districts Programme.

### Interlinking between Inclusiveness and Sustainability

1. **Inequality weakens long-term economic growth**
2. **Environmental degradation hits the poorest hardest** - Eg- Disaster induced Migration
3. **Inclusive growth strengthens environmental stewardship**
4. **Sustainable livelihoods reduce vulnerability**
5. **Intergenerational equity depends on both**

### Challenges to Inclusive Growth under a Market Economy

1. **Rising inequality** - Eg- the top 1% control 40% of net personal wealth.
2. **Regional disparities** due to unequal investment and infrastructure. Eg- BIMARU States
3. **Jobless growth** - Service sector contributes 55% of GDP but employs less than 30% workforce
4. **Weak social protection** for informal workers (over 85% of India's workforce).
5. **Market failures in public goods**. Eg- **Digital Apartheid** in Education

### Way Forward

1. **Capability Approach (Amartya Sen)** - increase Education and health spending to 6% and 2.5% of GDP respectively
2. Strengthen **progressive taxes**, wealth taxes and targeted subsidies to reduce income inequality and expand welfare spending.
3. **Align national policies with Paris Agreement targets**
4. **Universalise social security**, pensions, maternity benefits, and unemployment allowance

**A nexus approach towards sustainability and inclusiveness is needed for 'Sabka Saath, Sabka Vikas.'**
