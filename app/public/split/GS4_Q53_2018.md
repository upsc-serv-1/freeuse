## Question 53 (Year: 2018 | Paper: GS IV)

**Question ID: 2018-gs4-q53-civilsdaily**

**2018 - “In doing a good thing, everything is permitted which is not prohibited expressly or by clear implication”. Examine the statement with suitable examples in the context of a public servant discharging his/her duties.** [Year: 2018] [Marks: 10]

### Answer

This statement reflects the principle of **Administrative Discretion** and **Innovation** in public service. It also highlights the shift from **"Rule-based"** to **"Goal-oriented"** governance.

**Everything is permitted in doing good thing**

1. Adherence to the utilitarian **principle of ‘greatest happiness.’**

2. **Resource optimization-** Eg- **Armstrong Pame (IAS)** crowdfunded the "People’s Road".

3. **Innovation for reformative justice- Kiran Bedi (IPS)** introduced Vipassana.

4. **Behavioral Nudging (Gandhigiri)-** Using psychology instead of force. Eg- In the **Swachh Bharat Abhiyan**, officers gifted flowers to people defecating in the open.

5. Prioritising **social welfare** by bypassing bureaucratic rigidity. Eg- **Prashant Nair’s** "Operation Sulaimani" - Use of crowdsourced coupons to feed hunger

6. Finding **‘out of the box’ solutions**. Eg- IPS **chetan singh Rathore** using National Anthem to manage protesting crowd.

7. **Ethical use of discretion in crises**. Eg- **Dr. Syed Sehrish Asgar** used her office as a "human call center" during communication blackouts in J&K

8. Helps in **collaborative problem solving** rather than a top-down approach**. Eg- S.R. Sankaran** involved local community elders in identifying bonded laborers.

9. Creativity and innovations facilitate **adoption of technology** to solve problems. Eg- **Dr. Hari Chandana** building pavements from recycled plastic in Hyderabad.

10. **Inaction** out of fear of rules is often a greater "evil" than **Innovation** within the framework of the law. -leads to **policy paralysis**

### Boundaries that Need to be Followed

1. **Constitutional Morality-** One cannot "do good" by violating

Fundamental Rights. Eg- unauthorized phone tapping.

2. **Financial Propriety-** Eg- following **due process** in tender allocation

3. **Avoiding Conflict of Interest** through declaration and recusal

4. Acting in the **"spirit" of the law**

5. **Dignity and Human Rights-** Adherence to Kant’s categorical imperative.

6. **Non-arbitrariness** - No favouritism under the garb of welfare.

The ultimate test for any "permitted" action is **Mahatma Gandhi's Talisman**- "Will it lead to the upliftment of the poorest?"

### Counter-Argument- The Risks of Unchecked Discretion

1. Erosion of Procedural Due Process- Eg- 2G spectrum case
2. Financial Irregularity and Corruption
3. "Rule of Men" over "Rule of Law." Eg- Bulldozer justice
4. Violation of Fundamental Rights- Eg- custodial torture
5. Policy Instability and Lack of Continuity
6. Risk of Political Misuse

**7. Moral Hazard** of **normalising shortcuts.** Eg- Illegal tapping to catch criminals.

8. **Accountability Gap may lead to institutional Decay**

9. **The "Savior Complex"-** can lead to administrative arrogance.
