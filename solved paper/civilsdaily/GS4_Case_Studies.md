### Case Study 2 (Year: 2025)

In line with the Directive Principles of State Policy enshrined in the Indian Constitution, the government has a constitutional obligation to ensure basic needs - Roti, Kapda aur Makan (Food, Clothes and Shelter) - for the under-privileged. Pursuing this mandate, the district administration proposed clearing a portion of forest land to develop housing for the homeless and economically weaker sections of the society. The proposed land, however, is an ecologically sensitive zone densely populated with age-old trees, medicinal plants and vital biodiversity. Besides, these forests help to regulate micro-climate and rainfalls, provide habitat for wildlife, support soil fertility and prevent land/soil erosion and sustain livelihoods of tribal and nomadic communities. In spite of the ecological and social costs, the administration argues in favour of the said proposal by highlighting that this very initiative addresses fundamental human rights as a critical welfare priority. Besides, it fulfils the government’s duty to uplift & empower the poor through inclusive housing development. Further, these forest areas have become unsafe due to wild-animal threats and recurring human-wildlife conflicts. Lastly, clearing forest-zones may help to curb anti-social elements allegedly using these areas as hideouts, thereby enhancing law and order.

**(a) Can deforestation be ethically justified in the pursuit of social welfare objectives like housing for the homeless?

**(b) What are the socio-economic, administrative and ethical challenges in balancing environmental conservation with human development?

**(c) What substantial alternatives or policy interventions can be proposed to ensure that both environmental integrity and human dignity are protected?
The case presents a conflict between Articles 38, 39, 41 and 47 under **DPSP and the **constitutional duty of environmental protection under Articles 48A and 51A(g).
**Guiding Principle - “Sustainable Justice with Least Ecological Harm.”

**1. Ethical Justification for deforestation
**Justification

a. Last-resort necessity - Eg- Rehabilitation housing after **Uttarakhand floods (2013) used limited forest fringes.

b. Right to shelter priority - Eg- **PM Awas Yojana (PMAY) sites in semi-forested areas for landless poor.

c. Human Safety- Eliminating &quot;unsafe&quot; zones reduces human-wildlife conflict and crime.

d. Minimal clearance principle - Eg- Eco-hamlets in **Sikkim with limited tree felling.

e. Compensatory afforestation - Equal or greater reforestation elsewhere.

f. Prior informed consent of tribals - Respect for forest rights under **FRA, 2006.

g. Net social benefit test - If homelessness harm outweighs ecological loss.

h. Eco-sensitive construction - Green housing rather than concrete sprawl.
**Argument against

a. Inter-generational injustice - Future generations lose ecological assets.

b. Intrinsic Value of Nature- Biodiversity has a &quot;Right to Exist&quot; independent of human utility

c. Loss of **Ecosystem Services like rain, clean air that is more valuable than a house.

d. Displacement without consent violates **tribal rights

e. Non-Sustainability - Eg- **Amazon Rainforest clearing has led to decline in rainfall

f. Availability of alternatives - If other land exists, forest clearance is unethical.

g. Disaster risk - Eg- Landslides in **Himachal and Uttarakhand after forest clearing

**2. Challenges in balancing environmental conservation with human development
**Socio-economic challenges

1. Displacement of tribal communities - Loss of livelihoods and culture.

2. Human-wildlife conflict escalation - Habitat fragmentation increases clashes.

3. Livelihood Displacement- Losing minor forest produce. Eg- Kendu leaf collectors in Odisha).

4. Cultural Loss- Destruction of &quot;Sacred Groves&quot; and medicinal knowledge.
**Administrative challenges

1. Weak land-use planning - Poor zoning leading to forest encroachment.

2. Siloed Governance - Lack of synergy between forest, housing and revenue departments.

3. Law Enforcement- Eg- Naxal-affected Red Corridor areas.

4. Corruption/land mafia - Eg- Encroachments in **Aravalli range (Haryana-Rajasthan).

5. Land Scarcity- Lack of non-forest government land in hilly or tribal districts. Eg- Land constraints in Sikkim or Nagaland.

6. Implementation Gaps- Under utilization of CAMPA funds. (CAG report).
**Ethical challenges

1. Development vs Conservation - Welfare of today vs rights of tomorrow.

2. Anthropocentrism vs Ecocentrism - Human priority vs nature’s intrinsic value.

3. Rights-based dilemma - Right to housing vs right to clean environment.

4. Utilitarian vs Deontological ethics - Maximum good vs moral duty to nature.

5. Justice as Fairness- Is it fair to house the homeless by displacing the tribals?

6. Rights of animals - further fragmentation of habitats
**Policy interventions for balancing environmental integrity and human dignity

a. Use degraded non-forest land first - Brownfield sites over pristine forests.

b. Vertical affordable housing - Eg- **Mumbai SRA model for EWS housing.

c. In-situ slum redevelopment - Eg- **Delhi JJ cluster redevelopment.

d. Agro-Forestry Housing and &quot;Eco-Villages.&quot; Eg- Auroville, Pondicherry.

e. Use idle government land - Railways/Defence/Revenue land.

f. Satellite town planning - Planned peri-urban expansion. Eg- **Navi Mumbai model.

g. Use of **sustainable materials (bamboo, recycled waste) to reduce construction impact. Eg- Kerala’s Laurie Baker style.

h. Strict FRA, 2006 compliance - Prior informed consent of tribals mandatory.

i. Robust EIA + public hearings for transparent decision-making.

j. Human-wildlife conflict mitigation - Solar fencing, corridors, early warning.

k. Implement **&quot;Zoning&quot; where &quot;Ecologically Sensitive Area 1&quot; is strictly off-limits. (Gadgil Committee) As the **Supreme Court noted in the **T.N. Godavarman case, the &quot;Precautionary Principle&quot; must prevail. True social welfare lies in **Sustainable Habitat, not just concrete blocks.

• Cons - none significant; this is a complementary step.

Refuse the settlement, since

a. Criminal sexual offences are non-compoundable and any 'settlement' has no legal force,

b. Silence emboldens future harassment

c. The integrity of the justice process cannot be auctioned.
